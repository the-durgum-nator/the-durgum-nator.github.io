Artifical intelligence.

It's a doozy of a topic no matter how you slice it.
Once the moment we designed the first computer, people had thought of if, and maybe when, we'd turn them sentient.
And unfortunately, the version we have is not as sauve as J.A.R.V.I.S nor as intimidiating as AM.
We've got the GPTs, the Claudes, the Geminis, etc.

The technology isn't really that "insane" as our modern AI architecture that powers some of those previously mentioned (the Transformer) 
<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762"> can be fully summed up in 15 pages...</a>
and then for the entirity of field can be <a href="http://ema.cri-info.cm/wp-content/uploads/2019/07/2019BurkovTheHundred-pageMachineLearning.pdf"> done in a simple hundred pages., as per Burkov's book on the matter.</a>
<br>

Granted, a lot of work over the last 20 years in modern AI development only got recognized atfer the Attention paper hit public news, Wikipedia details how turbulent the progress in AI development has been for decades now. <br>
Compared to other topics, it's fair to say we've just cracked the surface of AI development.


However, its rapid "adoption" and integration into our society is quite worrying as an individual.<br>
Most of AI development stems from greedy folk overstepping moral and ethical boundaries for their own gain and to detriment of humanity as a whole. Building more monoliths yet squeezing more out the earth, just to grow themselves for ego and vanity, by shelling trillions into technology that's way more utopian in both meanings of the word while not tackling actual problems with it.    
<br> 
As much as lofty as that sounds, it's pretty down to earth compared to what some tech-bros are saying nowadays.
<br>


It'd be easy to avoid it, but unfortunately as someone who works in the field, it's important to have a stance on the topic.
Thankfully, my opinion can be broken up into 3 points:

1. As a hobbyist, AI can enable folk to do more... but often times removes the *fun* in doing so.<br>
A part of hobbyism is [doing] "for the fun of it", and so involving a non-human element to it feels hollow.<br>
Not to say the ability to research effectively with often the "average" answer being quite enough for most people is bad, but I tend to use AI as a "let's consult it", rather than "let's rely on it."

2. As a employee, AI can do similar, but enables management to not care about the human element being done.<br/>
Where as a software engineer/worker, your skillset gets devalued and thus your union card of skills gets looked down upon, as where as a manager, your employees get devalued, it's far easier for you to devalue others because of AI. Not to say knowing how to write basic loops is a good basis to test off of, but you get the gist.


3. As a human viewing this in a societal manner, AI is terrifying honestly. I don't see a pleasant outcome from this technology, and I don't think that's a good sign. Maybe it's my pessimisism popping through, but it's not like avoiding glaring issues while further expanding it isn't giving optimism a good shot at life here. Centralizing the "do-it-all" technology without restraint is a one-way ticket to abuse-of-power.

Personally, local model hosting/development has gotten good enough that it gives a bit more hope for AI development; a lot of the "concern" regarding environmental and development problems are much better given local models for context.
Plus I personally don't mind it using it as a concept, as long as the task at hand gets completed with minimal slow down.
<br/>But I am concerned for the future of developers, hobbyists, and us if we keep down this path with no regard for people first. Technology is not a thing guarenteed to be the next thing to hop onto. Just ask the Luddites.
